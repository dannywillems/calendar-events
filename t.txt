❯ I'm an expert in cryptography, I would like to make some side hustle by

● Good pivot, and you've already built the targeting tool for it. Here's how I'd
  think about it, specific to a cryptographer who also has internet-scale
  measurement data.

  The core model: conferences are the shop window, not the product

  The money in this side hustle is rarely the conference honorarium. It splits
  into three tiers, lowest to highest paid:

  1. Invited/keynote talks — usually travel + hotel, sometimes a €1-3k
  honorarium at industry events. Academic ones (Real World Crypto, Eurocrypt,
  CRYPTO) pay nothing and you often pay to attend. Their value is reputation,
  not cash.
  2. Paid trainings at commercial cons — this is the direct earner. You teach a
  1-2 day class, the con sells seats, you take a revenue share. A sold-out 2-day
  class can net roughly €8-30k depending on the con's cut and seat price. This
  is real but lumpy.
  3. Private corporate trainings — the actual income. Companies who see your
  conference talk/training book you on-site at €4-12k/day. The conference is
  lead-gen for this.

  So the strategy is: talk to build name → train at cons to prove you can teach 
  → convert into private corporate bookings. Optimize for tier 3; tiers 1-2 are
  the funnel.

  Your differentiator

  Two angles, and you can sell both:

  - "Cryptography failures at internet scale." Almost nobody teaching applied
  crypto has their own measurement data. You can build a hands-on training
  around real broken TLS, exposed keys, weak PKI, misconfigured protocols you
  observe from scanning. That's a class no other trainer can copy, and it sells
  to both red-team and blue-team audiences. This is your moat.
  - Post-quantum migration. NIST finalized ML-KEM / ML-DSA / SLH-DSA (FIPS
  203/204/205) in 2024, and enterprises are now doing crypto-agility and
  "harvest-now-decrypt-later" risk work. There's well-funded demand for someone
  who can run a credible PQC migration workshop. As a working cryptographer you
  have the credibility most security trainers lack.

  Package these as two distinct 1-2 day courses with explicit learning outcomes,
  prerequisites, and a lab setup. That artifact is what conferences and
  companies actually buy.

  Which conferences in your calendar to target (and how)

  The ones that pay trainers via a Call for Trainings (CFT), filter your
  calendar to kind = industry / community in Europe:

  - Black Hat (USA / Europe / MEA / Asia) — the gold standard for paid
  trainings; per-seat revenue share, large audiences, premium pricing. Their CFT
  opens months ahead.
  - Troopers (Heidelberg), Nullcon (Berlin), hardwear.io (Amsterdam, ideal for
  your side-channel/crypto-hardware angle), OffensiveCon (Berlin), 44CON
  (London), DeepSec (Vienna), Insomni'hack (Lausanne), NorthSec (Montreal).
  - BruCON (Mechelen) — local to you in Belgium. Lowest travel cost, highest
  relationship ROI. Start here.
  - SANS runs a structured (well-paid) instructor path, but they own the
  courseware and the bar is high; treat it as a separate long-game track.

  For reputation that drives bookings: get accepted talks at Real World Crypto
  and Eurocrypt — they don't pay, but the people in the room are exactly who
  hire cryptography trainers and consultants. FOSDEM (free, Brussels, huge) and
  CCC (not yet in your calendar — worth adding) are high-visibility, low-cost.

  For enterprise/vertical lead-gen (your audits/workshops side): kind = business
  events — it-sa, GISEC, FS-ISAC, GITEX — are where buyers are. You're less
  likely to be paid to speak there, but an invited talk or sponsor workshop
  converts to corporate work.
